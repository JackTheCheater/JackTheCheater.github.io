Waste Management

Poor waste management contributes to climate change and air pollution and directly affects many ecosystems and species. Waste management is broken down into six types of treatment: landfilling, incineration with energy recovery, incineration without energy recovery, recycling, backfilling and other disposals. A landfill (disposal category) is a site to collect and store waste that is going to be transported and treated. Landfills release methane, the very powerful greenhouse gas linked to climate change. Methane is formed by microorganisms present in landfills from biodegradable waste, such as food, paper and garden waste. Depending on the way they are built, landfills might also contaminate soil and water, so it's important to reduce them as much as possible. Incineration is the process of combustion that converts waste into ash, flue gas and heat. It releases gases that contribute to air pollution and the greenhouse effect. Through this process, is possible to produce electric energy (recovery category). When the production of electric energy from incineration is not possible, it's classified in the disposal category. Recycling (corresponding category) is the process of conversion of waste into new materials and objects. This practice reduces the consumption of fresh raw materials, energy use, greenhouse gas emissions, air pollution (from incineration) and water pollution (from landfilling). Backfilling (reuse category) is the process to transform waste into new products without pre-treatments. Backfilling and recycling are reinforced by waste sorting. This fact explains the importance of the circular economy. A circular economy is an eco-friendly model of production and consumption which can regenerate on its own forever.
