Green Areas

The trees of the forests take up carbon dioxide from the atmosphere and release oxygen during photosynthesis. They also carry out the opposite process, also known as respiration, but when forests are growing, photosynthesis exceeds respiration, and the surplus carbon is stored in tree trunks, roots and soil. This is called "sequestration". When forests are cut down, much of that stored carbon is released into the atmosphere again as carbon dioxide. This is how deforestation and forest degradation contribute to global warming. Here's a choropleth map showing the amount of forests in European countries expressed in hectares (ha). Move the cursor on the countries to see details. Choose the year using the bottom.

Sweden, Spain and Finland are the countries with the highest amount of forests. In general, all European countries increased the amount of forests, but France is the country which registered the most important increase in wooded areas from 1990 to 2020.
Utilised agricultural areas (UAA) can be classified into three types: low intensity, medium intensity and high intensity. A UAA is classified under the class "low intensity" if the amount of money spent for inputs is lower than 88 euros/hectare. A UAA is classified under the class "medium intensity" if the amount of money spent for inputs is between 88 euros/hectares and 560 euros/hectare. A UAA is classified under the class "high intensity" if the amount of money spent for inputs is greater than 560 euros/hectare. The inputs are fertilisers, soil improvers, pesticides and other means of protection such as traps and baits, bird scarers, anti-hail shells, frost protection and purchased feed. These kinds of inputs are toxic and they damage the soil, the plants and the animals, so it's necessary to use them as little as possible. From the choropleth map above, it's possible to observe that from 2010 to 2019, many European countries increased the amount of high-intensity UAA, instead of reducing them.
